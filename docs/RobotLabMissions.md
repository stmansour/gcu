# Robot Lab Missions
## Chapter Guide / Curriculum Specification v1.0

**Project:** Grandpa's Creative Universe (GCU)  
**Module:** Robot Lab  
**Purpose:** Define the intended learning arc, gameplay focus, and mission ideas for the major chapters of the Robot Lab game.  
**Audience:** Primarily children old enough to follow guided experimentation, with increasing depth over time  
**Design Principle:** Every chapter should teach a real engineering idea through play, visible cause/effect, and helping SWIRL-E improve.

---

# 1. Overview

Robot Lab is a guided, playful robotics-building experience in which the child helps bring **SWIRL-E** to life one system at a time.

The game should not feel like a textbook or formal lesson. It should feel like:

- fixing
- building
- experimenting
- testing
- discovering

Each chapter should follow the same broad pattern:

1. SWIRL-E has a problem
2. The child is given a hands-on mission
3. The child experiments with a system
4. The game shows visible consequences
5. Success improves SWIRL-E in a meaningful way
6. A short explanation reinforces the idea after the puzzle is solved

---

# 2. Chapter Design Rules

Each chapter should follow these rules:

## 2.1 Start with a story problem
The player should understand what is wrong with SWIRL-E.

## 2.2 Make the mechanic tactile and visual
The child should move, adjust, wire, place, tune, or assemble things directly.

## 2.3 Show invisible systems visibly
Electricity, focus, sound, logic, and balance should be shown through animations and effects.

## 2.4 Make mistakes informative and fun
Wrong answers should not just be “incorrect.” They should produce interesting outcomes.

## 2.5 Make success emotionally meaningful
Each successful chapter should clearly improve SWIRL-E’s abilities.

## 2.6 Favor intuition before explanation
Let the child see and feel what works before giving the short science/engineering summary.

# 3. Chapter 1 — Electricity

## Status
This chapter is already implemented.

## Purpose
Introduce the idea that electrical components affect current flow in different ways.

## Story
SWIRL-E’s eye module needs power.

## Core Learning Concepts

- electricity flows through circuits
- too much current can damage a component
- resistors limit current
- inductors and capacitors behave differently from resistors
- correct circuit design matters

## Gameplay Pattern

- player chooses or routes components into the circuit
- simulation runs visually
- electrons flow through the circuit
- component behavior is shown qualitatively
- wrong solutions create interesting failures
- correct solution powers the eyes safely

## Emotional Payoff
SWIRL-E wakes up and can see.

## Notes
This chapter establishes the main learning pattern for Robot Lab:
**experiment first, understand second.**

# 4. Chapter 2 — Optics

## Purpose
Teach that seeing clearly depends on properly focusing light.

## Story
SWIRL-E’s eyes now have power, but everything is blurry. His lenses are out of calibration.

## Core Learning Concepts

- light can be focused
- lenses bend light
- focus depends on distance and lens position
- blurry vision and sharp vision are not random; they depend on alignment

## Possible Mission Ideas

### Mission 2.1 — Focus the Lens
The child slides a lens forward and backward until an image becomes sharp.

### Mission 2.2 — Match the Right Lens
The child chooses between lens types to bring an object into focus.

### Mission 2.3 — Align Both Eyes
The child must tune both left and right optical paths so SWIRL-E can see properly.

## Gameplay Pattern

- show a blurry object or scene
- child adjusts lens position, spacing, or lens choice
- visual feedback changes continuously as focus improves
- successful alignment makes the target image sharp

## Visual Opportunities

- blur to sharp transition
- moving focal point indicators
- funny distorted views when lens is wrong
- SWIRL-E’s eyes adjusting mechanically

## Emotional Payoff
SWIRL-E can now see clearly and react to what he sees.

# 5. Chapter 3 — Color Sensing

## Purpose
Teach that color can be measured and interpreted through sensor channels.

## Story
SWIRL-E can see shapes, but his colors are wrong. He thinks bananas are blue and grass is purple.

## Core Learning Concepts

- color can be broken into channels
- sensors can misread or miscalibrate color
- red, green, and blue combine to form many colors
- sensing and interpretation are separate things

## Possible Mission Ideas

### Mission 3.1 — Calibrate RGB Sensors
The child adjusts red, green, and blue sensitivity sliders until colors look correct.

### Mission 3.2 — Sort by Color
The child helps SWIRL-E identify objects by correcting color readings.

### Mission 3.3 — Find the Hidden Object
Correct color calibration reveals an object that is otherwise hard to distinguish.

## Gameplay Pattern

- scene begins with wrong color balance
- child adjusts channels or sensor calibration
- image updates in real time
- correct tuning restores natural-looking colors

## Visual Opportunities

- exaggerated wrong colors
- live RGB overlays
- false-color scan mode
- color bars or sensor dials

## Emotional Payoff
SWIRL-E can correctly identify colorful objects in the world.

---

# 6. Chapter 4 — Motors

## Purpose
Teach that motors convert electricity into movement and must be driven appropriately.

## Story
SWIRL-E can see now, but he can’t move his head, turn his wheels, or raise his arms correctly.

## Core Learning Concepts

- motors create motion
- too little power causes stalling
- too much power causes jerky or unsafe behavior
- gearing, speed, and torque involve tradeoffs

## Possible Mission Ideas

### Mission 4.1 — Spin the Eye Motor
Get a small motor to move SWIRL-E’s eye assembly smoothly.

### Mission 4.2 — Raise the Arm
Tune the motor power so SWIRL-E can lift an arm without overshooting.

### Mission 4.3 — Drive the Wheels
Balance speed and control so SWIRL-E moves properly.

## Gameplay Pattern

- child adjusts motor power or driver settings
- movement is visibly too weak, too strong, or just right
- correct setup produces smooth and useful movement

## Visual Opportunities

- stalled motor shaking
- overshoot and wobble
- smooth controlled movement
- spinning gears and power indicators

## Emotional Payoff
SWIRL-E gains real physical movement.

---

# 7. Chapter 5 — Control Systems

## Purpose
Teach that movement and stability often require feedback and adjustment.

## Story
SWIRL-E can move, but he is clumsy. He overcorrects, wobbles, or falls over.

## Core Learning Concepts

- sensors can detect motion and position
- a system can correct itself using feedback
- too much correction causes oscillation
- too little correction causes drift and instability

## Possible Mission Ideas

### Mission 5.1 — Balance the Head
Tune head stabilization so SWIRL-E’s vision stays steady.

### Mission 5.2 — Keep Him Standing
Adjust a balance system so SWIRL-E does not tip over.

### Mission 5.3 — Steady the Camera
Tune stabilization while SWIRL-E moves over uneven ground.

## Gameplay Pattern

- child adjusts control parameters or feedback strength
- system responds visibly
- bad settings make SWIRL-E wobble or oscillate
- good settings stabilize him

## Visual Opportunities

- wobbling robot body
- live balance bars
- gyro indicators
- exaggerated oscillation when wrong

## Emotional Payoff
SWIRL-E becomes steady and more capable.

# 8. Chapter 6 — Sound

## Purpose
Teach that sound is a physical signal that can be sensed, amplified, and interpreted.

## Story
SWIRL-E can see and move, but he can’t hear properly. Sounds are too faint, too loud, or distorted.

## Core Learning Concepts

- microphones detect sound
- signals can be amplified
- too much gain causes distortion or feedback
- sound has pitch, loudness, and pattern

## Possible Mission Ideas

### Mission 6.1 — Turn On the Ears
Get the microphone system working.

### Mission 6.2 — Tune the Gain
Adjust sound sensitivity so SWIRL-E can hear a voice clearly.

### Mission 6.3 — Separate the Signals
Help SWIRL-E distinguish one important sound from background noise.

## Gameplay Pattern

- child adjusts gain, filter, or placement
- visual sound waves respond in real time
- wrong settings cause weak hearing or distortion
- correct settings make sound clear

## Visual Opportunities

- waveform animations
- pulsing sound rings
- feedback squeal moments
- voice/sound icons lighting up

## Emotional Payoff
SWIRL-E can hear and respond to sound.

# 9. Chapter 7 — Logic

## Purpose
Teach that behavior can be built from rules, conditions, and simple decision making.

## Story
SWIRL-E has sensors, but he doesn’t know what to do with the information yet.

## Core Learning Concepts

- rules can drive behavior
- conditions lead to actions
- logic determines responses
- simple systems can create useful intelligence

## Possible Mission Ideas

### Mission 7.1 — If This, Then That
Set up a rule: if obstacle detected, stop.

### Mission 7.2 — Choose the Correct Path
Combine two or more conditions to guide SWIRL-E.

### Mission 7.3 — Build a Behavior Chain
Create a small sequence of sensing and response steps.

## Gameplay Pattern

- child assembles simple rule blocks or behavior nodes
- system runs the behavior
- incorrect rules create funny or useless behavior
- correct logic lets SWIRL-E complete a goal

## Visual Opportunities

- block-style logic pieces
- glowing decision paths
- sensor icons feeding into action icons
- animated “thinking” indicators

## Emotional Payoff
SWIRL-E begins to act intelligently.

# 10. Chapter 8 — Navigation

## Purpose
Teach that moving through the world requires sensing, planning, and obstacle handling.

## Story
SWIRL-E can think now, but he still gets lost or bumps into things.

## Core Learning Concepts

- navigation depends on information about the environment
- paths can be planned
- obstacles require rerouting
- sensing and motion must work together

## Possible Mission Ideas

### Mission 8.1 — Reach the Charging Station
Help SWIRL-E navigate to a visible goal.

### Mission 8.2 — Avoid Obstacles
Plan or tune behavior to get around barriers.

### Mission 8.3 — Follow the Trail
Use markers or sensor cues to guide SWIRL-E along a path.

## Gameplay Pattern

- child helps define or influence path selection
- navigation attempt is shown
- poor choices cause collisions or wandering
- good choices get SWIRL-E to the target efficiently

## Visual Opportunities

- route overlays
- sensor pings
- path nodes
- minimap or floor-grid views

## Emotional Payoff
SWIRL-E can get where he needs to go.

# 11. Chapter 9 — Power Management

## Purpose
Teach that energy is limited and must be used wisely.

## Story
SWIRL-E works now, but he runs out of power too quickly or wastes energy.

## Core Learning Concepts

- batteries store limited energy
- systems consume different amounts of power
- efficiency matters
- engineering often requires tradeoffs

## Possible Mission Ideas

### Mission 9.1 — Make the Battery Last
Choose settings that reduce waste.

### Mission 9.2 — Prioritize the Systems
Decide which systems need full power and which can run in low-power mode.

### Mission 9.3 — Survive the Mission
Manage energy well enough for SWIRL-E to complete a longer task.

## Gameplay Pattern

- child allocates power or adjusts system use
- battery meter drains visibly
- inefficient choices fail early
- balanced choices let mission succeed

## Visual Opportunities

- battery meter and heat/waste indicators
- power flow overlays
- “high power” vs “eco mode” visuals
- emergency low-power warnings

## Emotional Payoff
SWIRL-E becomes reliable and efficient.

# 12. Chapter 10 — System Integration

## Purpose
Teach that a real robot is a system made of many subsystems working together.

## Story
SWIRL-E now has all his major subsystems, but they need to work together for a real mission.

## Core Learning Concepts

- real systems are combinations of many smaller systems
- success often depends on coordination
- a failure in one subsystem can affect the whole robot
- engineering is about integration, not just individual parts

## Possible Mission Ideas

### Mission 10.1 — The Full Test
SWIRL-E must see, move, balance, and think in one sequence.

### Mission 10.2 — Rescue Task
SWIRL-E must locate an item, navigate to it, and interact with it.

### Mission 10.3 — Final Build Challenge
The player configures multiple systems and then watches the robot attempt a meaningful mission.

## Gameplay Pattern

- several systems interact in one mission
- player uses knowledge from prior chapters
- failures reveal which subsystem is weak
- success feels like the child truly built a robot

## Visual Opportunities

- multi-stage mission screen
- subsystem health/status indicators
- integrated success sequence
- SWIRL-E using all his abilities

## Emotional Payoff
The child feels that they truly helped build a complete robot.

# 13. Multiple Small Missions Strategy

Robot Lab should not rely only on one large mission per chapter. A better structure is often:

- **1 major chapter**
- **2 to 4 small missions inside it**

Benefits:

- frequent victories
- less frustration
- easier pacing
- more room for experimentation
- better fit for children’s attention spans

Recommended structure:

- Mission A: introduction
- Mission B: variation
- Mission C: slightly harder integration
- Optional Mission D: playful bonus challenge

---

# 14. Difficulty Progression

Difficulty should increase by:

- adding one new idea at a time
- increasing integration between systems
- reducing hand-holding gradually
- preserving visual clarity even as complexity increases

The game should never become abstract too quickly.

---

# 15. Core Tone for All Chapters

Every chapter should feel like this:

- SWIRL-E is improving
- the child is helping
- engineering is playful
- mistakes are interesting
- discovery is rewarding

The child should feel:

> “I am building a robot.”

not:

> “I am taking a lesson.”
